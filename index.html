Analytics / Data Analytics
Data · Business intelligence · Modeling
#1 · 55.4%
By far the largest functional destination for MSBA graduates — more than half of all placements, and the leading function in every cohort year. Graduates land as data analysts, senior data analysts, business analysts, business intelligence analysts, and data scientists across nearly every industry. This is the core of what the MSBA prepares you for: turning data into decisions. Your edge is the combination of technical depth (SQL, Python/R, modeling, visualization) and the business framing to make analysis matter to stakeholders.

Financial services · Risk · Corporate finance
#3 · 9.4%

The tiering system: Tier 1 are proven MSBA employers with repeated hiring history (5+ hires) — research these first. Tier 2 are firms with a real but smaller MSBA track record (2–4 hires). Beyond these, build a Tier 3 of regional "hidden gems" — analytics-hiring companies of roughly 500–5,000 employees with analyst, rotational, or decision-science programs, especially across the Carolinas, Virginia, and Georgia. Build your target list across all three, but start where the MSBA track record is strongest. Past hiring does not guarantee future openings.

              Timing tracks for the MSBA search
              After you build the personalized plan above, choose the time-to-offer track that matches your readiness and recruiting calendar. These tracks remain useful for deciding pace, volume, and escalation points.
              Track A · Fast-Track
              Offer by January (within ~6 months). For students with strong technical depth coming in (SQL, Python, prior analytics internships) or a clear, well-articulated transferable narrative. Convert existing readiness.
              Track B · Standard
              Offer by May (within ~10 months). The typical MSBA profile: building technical fluency through the program while sharpening positioning, portfolio, and networking. Build, then convert. Most students belong here.
              Track C · Career Changer
              Offer by November after graduation (within ~16 months). For limited-experience, nontraditional, or international students who may use post-graduation search time. Not a failure plan — a development plan.
              Track A — Fast-Track (offer by January, ~6 months in). Best fit if you have relevant internships or co-ops, an undergraduate major that aligns with your goal, or a transferable story you can articulate convincingly (e.g., Biology → Healthcare Consulting, Psychology → HR / Consumer Insights, Engineering → Operations, Communications → Sales Leadership, Political Science / History → Consulting & Research). The goal in July is not exploration — it's positioning. Apply and network in parallel from week one; there is no isolated "prep phase" you can afford.

              January targets: 1 offer · 3+ final-round interviews · 10+ first-round interviews. Use your strongest portfolio project (a model, a dashboard, a SQL-heavy analysis), Practicum I, and your Machine Learning and Data Management coursework as interview evidence.
              Goal: a decided target and live applications by the end of week three — no exploration luxury.
              • Commit to one function and 2 industries. Use the Function and Industry sections above; pick the pathway with the most placements that you can credibly tell a story about. Indecision is the single biggest killer of a 6-month search.
              • Build a 40-employer target list in 48 hours. Start from the Employers section, then mine 12Twenty for every firm that has hired your target role. Sort into "warm" (WFUSB has hired there) and "cold."
              • Rewrite your resume once, correctly. One master resume framed to the target function, quantified bullets, ATS-clean. Get it reviewed by your coach this week — not in three rounds over a month.
              • Start applying day one. Don't wait to feel ready. Submit 5+ applications in week one to warm-list employers so the funnel is already filling while you sharpen everything else.
              Goal: maximum qualified applications, each backed by a referral wherever possible.
              • 10–12 targeted applications per week. In a sprint, volume matters — but only to roles you actually fit. Track everything in one tracker with date, contact, and status.
              • Referral-first sequencing. Before applying cold, spend 10 minutes finding a WFUSB alum at that employer (use the Alumni buttons on each card and LinkedIn). A referred application converts several times better than a cold one — this is your highest-leverage move on a short clock.
              • Send 5 alumni outreach notes weekly. Short, specific, ask for 15 minutes. Reference shared WFUSB connection and one concrete question. Convert calls into referrals by ending with: "Is there someone on the hiring team you'd suggest I connect with?"
              • Batch your interview prep. Build a 6-story behavioral bank (STAR format) and, if consulting/finance, drill 8–10 cases or 1 technical guide. You're preparing while interviewing, not before.
              Goal: turn active processes into an offer and negotiate without over-deliberating.
              • Triage your funnel weekly with your coach. Concentrate energy on live processes; cut dead leads fast. A short search has no room for sunk-cost applications.
              • Run a tight follow-up cadence. 24-hour thank-you after every interview; check in every 5–7 business days on pending decisions. Polite persistence wins close calls.
              • Negotiate, briefly. Even on a fast timeline, ask once: "Is there flexibility on the base?" and use 12Twenty salary data as your anchor. One well-framed ask is standard and expected.
              • Have a fallback channel. If offers haven't materialized by week 20, widen geography (add Remote/Hybrid and adjacent metros) and adjacent roles rather than waiting on stalled processes.
              Track B — Standard (offer by May, ~10 months in). This fits the majority of MSBA students: you're building technical fluency through the program while you sharpen positioning, portfolio, and networking, and you may still be narrowing your target function. You have enough runway to explore briefly, build real analytics evidence, and run a disciplined search — but the MSBA is compressed, so phases overlap. The principle: networking and applications start by month two and never stop. The Analytics Practicum often becomes your single strongest interview example.

              By-graduation targets: 100–120 applications · ~100 networking conversations · 75 alumni conversations · 15–20 interviews · 5–10 final rounds · 1–3 offers.
              Goal: a data-informed target function, industry, and starter employer list before classes fully ramp.
              • Use this site's outcomes data to set direction. Cross-reference the Function, Industry, and Employer sections: where do placements actually exist for the work you want? Pick one primary function and two industries.
              • Meet your career coach in week one or two. Bring a draft target, not a blank slate. Coaches give sharper guidance when you've already done the first pass.
              • Build a 25–30 employer target list. Anchor it on the proven Employers section, then expand via 12Twenty and Handshake. Tag each as warm or cold.
              • Get your LinkedIn and resume to "good enough to send." Perfect is the enemy of started — you'll refine continuously, but the base must exist now.
              Goal: a credible proof point in your target area and a live, warming network — while early applications begin.
              • Create one portfolio-grade artifact. An end-to-end analytics project, a predictive model, a published Tableau/Power BI dashboard, or a SQL-heavy analysis — something that proves you can do the target work, usable in interviews and on LinkedIn/GitHub.
              • Start alumni outreach now: 3–5 per week. Use the Alumni buttons throughout this site to find WFUSB grads at target employers. Lead with a specific, 15-minute ask; always close by asking who else you should talk to.
              • Begin applying to early-posting roles. Consulting, finance, and rotational programs recruit early — don't wait for month four if your targets are open.
              • Attend every relevant employer event. Treat info sessions as networking, not information-gathering. Get one name and one follow-up from each.
              Goal: peak application volume backed by referrals, with interview skills fully sharp.
              • 6–8 targeted, referral-backed applications per week. Quality over spray-and-pray: every application should map to your target and, ideally, ride a warm introduction.
              • Finish a 6–8 story STAR behavioral bank. Cover leadership, conflict, failure, impact, teamwork. Rehearse out loud and with your coach until they're natural, not memorized.
              • Drill role-specific interviews. Analytics/data → a live SQL test, a take-home data challenge, and a portfolio walk-through; data science → modeling, statistics, and ML concept questions; consulting → analytics cases + structured problem-solving; finance/risk → modeling + domain questions. Mock with peers and coaches.
              • Convert every networking call into a referral or a name. By now your earlier outreach should be paying off in warm intros to live roles.
              Goal: honest assessment, then widen or sharpen based on real signal.
              • Run a mid-search audit with your coach. Getting interviews but not offers? It's interview skills. Not getting interviews? It's targeting, resume, or referral volume. Diagnose precisely.
              • Expand into adjacent roles and geographies if signal is thin. Add the Remote/Hybrid channel and second-choice functions where your story still holds.
              • Go two degrees deep in networking. Ask your strongest contacts for introductions to their colleagues — second-degree intros open the hidden job market.
              • Refresh your artifact and stories with new learning. Fold in anything sharp you've learned in interviews.
              Goal: close strong, negotiate well, and choose deliberately.
              • Prioritize live processes ruthlessly. Pour energy into final rounds; keep a thin pipeline of new applications as insurance.
              • Prep each final round like it's the only one. Company research, tailored questions, and a clear "why this firm, why this role, why me" narrative.
              • Negotiate with data. Anchor on 12Twenty salary outcomes for your role and metro. Ask once, professionally, and be ready to articulate your value.
              • Evaluate offers on a real rubric. Role trajectory, manager, learning, comp, location. Don't let relief stampede you into the first yes if a better-fit process is close.
              Track C — Career Changer (offer by November after graduation, ~16 months in). This is not a failure plan — it's a strategic development plan for students with limited experience, significant career-change goals, nontraditional or liberal-arts backgrounds needing stronger business translation, or international students entering an unfamiliar labor market (who may also use OPT/post-graduation search time). It runs the full 10-month program plus up to 6 months after graduation, focusing first on building employability, then converting. The risk is losing momentum after graduation — so the post-grad phase keeps hard weekly targets.

              By-graduation targets: 120–150 applications · 50+ networking contacts and 20 informational interviews by December · differentiate in spring with the Practicum, advanced electives, and certifications (SQL / Tableau / Power BI, a cloud credential like AWS/Azure, Python or ML certificates, SAS, or Google Analytics).
              Goal: a committed target, a credible proof point, and live applications well before graduation.
              • Commit to a target using outcomes data. Use the Function, Industry, and Employer sections to pick one primary function and two industries you can tell a credible story about.
              • Run a gap analysis against real job descriptions. Pull 10 postings for your goal role and close the recurring skill gaps — Excel/modeling for finance, SQL/Tableau for analytics, case frameworks for consulting, a brand/GTM project for marketing.
              • Build one portfolio-grade artifact and start outreach. A case, model, or project you can walk through in interviews; begin 3–5 alumni contacts per week using the Alumni buttons throughout this site.
              • Apply to early-cycle roles now. Consulting, finance, and rotational programs recruit early — don't wait. Get a referral-backed application in before winter.
              Goal: run the full intensive search through graduation, landing an offer if the timing aligns.
              • Sustain 6–8 referral-backed applications weekly. Quality over volume; every application maps to your target and ideally rides a warm intro.
              • Reach interview mastery. 20+ cases for consulting; full technical fluency for finance; a refined portfolio walk-through for marketing/analytics. Mock relentlessly with peers and your coach.
              • Network into the hidden market. Ask your strongest contacts for second-degree introductions — roles surface before they're posted.
              • Convert and negotiate if an offer lands. Prioritize live processes, prep each final round fully, and anchor any negotiation on 12Twenty salary data. If the right offer comes by graduation, take it — the post-grad runway is insurance, not a plan to coast.
              Goal: prevent the post-grad drop-off — the search is now your full-time job.
              • Treat the search as a 9-to-5. Without classes, structure is the risk. Block fixed daily hours for applications, outreach, and interview prep, and keep your tracker current.
              • Stay engaged with your career coach. WFUSB coaching continues after graduation — schedule standing check-ins so someone still sees your pipeline weekly.
              • Widen the aperture. Add the Remote/Hybrid channel, adjacent functions where your story holds, and off-cycle employers that hire year-round rather than on a campus calendar.
              • Re-warm dormant contacts. Send a brief "here's where I landed in my search / still targeting X" update to everyone you spoke with in the spring — it reactivates referrals.
              Goal: convert an off-cycle or held-out opportunity into the right offer and finish the search.
              • Concentrate on live processes. By now your network and reps should be producing real interviews; manage them like a portfolio and pour energy into final rounds.
              • Leverage your matured network for advocacy. Months of relationship-building mean you likely have a champion inside target firms — ask them to speak up in the hiring debrief.
              • Negotiate from a position of patience. The extra runway lets you hold out for genuine fit — use competing timelines respectfully and anchor on 12Twenty data.
              • Set a firm decision deadline. An open-ended timeline breeds indecision; commit to a date by which you'll accept the best available offer so the search has a real finish line.
              The January checkpoint
              Mid-year, honestly grade your pipeline and act on the signal. Where you land determines what changes next.
              Green — on track
              3+ active interviews, recruiter engagement, a strong pipeline. Keep going — maintain weekly volume and focus energy on converting live processes.
              Yellow — networking, few interviews
              Conversations are happening but interviews aren't. Increase networking by ~50%, prioritize referral-backed applications, and pressure-test your resume and targeting with your coach.
              Red — no traction
              No interviews, no recruiter engagement. Immediate intervention: resume rewrite, employer retargeting, an alumni-referral campaign, and geographic expansion. Consider whether a longer track (B→C) fits your reality.
              The one rule across every plan: applications and networking run in parallel, never in sequence. The highest-probability path isn't "apply everywhere" — it's to target employers with demonstrated MSBA hiring history, build relationships with their alumni and recruiters early (summer–fall), and enter peak recruiting season already known by the employer.
